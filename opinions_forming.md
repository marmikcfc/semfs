# How Humans Form Opinions: A Layered Mental Model from Neurons to Justification

## TL;DR
- Humans do not form opinions by neutrally weighing evidence and then concluding; the dominant scientific picture across neuroscience, psychology, and philosophy is the reverse — a fast, automatic, emotion-laden intuition arrives first (the brain's "best guess"), and conscious reasoning largely arrives second to justify and defend it, especially for socially or identity-charged topics.
- A single mechanism recurs at every level of magnification: the brain is a *prediction-and-error-correction machine* that updates "beliefs" (priors) only when the surprise (prediction error) is large enough and the new information is not too threatening to the self or the group — which is why opinions feel like perceptions, why they resist disconfirmation, and why they function as badges of group membership.
- The layers are complementary, not competing: predictive processing/Bayesian-brain (neuroscience) → dual-process, heuristics, dissonance, and argumentative reasoning (cognitive psychology) → social identity, conformity, and cultural cognition (social psychology) → credences, justification, and the ethics of belief (epistemology), with evolutionary error-management logic explaining why a "biased" believer is often an adaptive one.

## Key Findings

**1. The "explain-like-I'm-5" core.** An opinion is your brain's *guess* about what something is like — is this food yummy or yucky, is this person nice or mean, is this idea good or bad. Your brain makes the guess super fast using old memories and feelings, and *then* you think up reasons for it. You usually keep your guess unless something surprises you a lot — and you change it more easily when changing it doesn't make you feel bad or let down your friends.

**2. Intuition first, reasoning second.** This is the most robustly replicated finding across fields. Haidt's social intuitionist model (introduced in "The Emotional Dog and Its Rational Tail: A Social Intuitionist Approach to Moral Judgment," *Psychological Review*, 2001, 108(4):814–834) casts reason as a *press secretary* (rider) for automatic intuition (elephant); Kahneman's System 1/System 2; and Mercier & Sperber's argumentative theory all converge on it.

**3. Reason evolved to win arguments, not to find truth.** Mercier & Sperber's argumentative theory (*The Enigma of Reason*, 2017) holds that reasoning is "first and foremost a social competence" — biased toward confirming the reasoner's view and lazy in scrutinizing it — which explains confirmation bias as a design feature, not a bug.

**4. Emotion is not the enemy of reason; it is a precondition for it.** Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis (*Descartes' Error*, 1994) shows that patients with ventromedial prefrontal damage, stripped of emotional "markers," become *unable to decide* — establishing that feeling is integrated into, not opposed to, judgment.

**5. Belief formation is neurally tied to emotion and reward.** Harris, Sheth & Cohen (*Annals of Neurology*, Feb 2008, 63(2):141–7), using fMRI in 14 adults, found that belief vs. disbelief is mediated by the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) — a region tied to emotion and reward — and concluded that "the involvement of the VMPFC in belief processing suggests an anatomical link between the purely cognitive aspects of belief and human emotion and reward." (Belief judgments were also faster — ~3.26 s — than disbelief, ~3.70 s, or uncertainty, ~3.66 s.) Westen et al. (2006) found politically motivated reasoning lit up emotion-regulation and reward circuits, *not* "cold" reasoning regions.

**6. Opinions are group-membership markers.** Social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner) and Kahan's identity-protective cognition show that on contested topics, people process information to protect standing in their group — making it individually rational to reject identity-threatening facts.

**7. The "backfire effect" is largely a myth.** A major reassessment — Wood & Porter, "The Elusive Backfire Effect: Mass Attitudes' Steadfast Factual Adherence" (*Political Behavior*, 2019, 41:135–163), spanning five experiments, 10,100+ subjects, and 52 issues — reported: "Across all experiments, we found no corrections capable of triggering backfire, despite testing precisely the kinds of polarized issues where backfire should be expected... By and large, citizens heed factual information, even when such information challenges their ideological commitments." This matters because it means the picture is not hopeless — facts *do* move people, just not when delivered in identity-threatening ways.

**8. Philosophy reframes the whole thing.** Epistemology distinguishes opinion from knowledge (justified true belief, complicated by Gettier), models belief as graded *credence* (Bayesian epistemology), and debates whether we can even *choose* beliefs (doxastic voluntarism) and whether believing on insufficient evidence is unethical (Clifford vs. James).

## Details

### The worked example (zooming in layer by layer)
Throughout, follow one concrete case: **You are at a friend's dinner party and are served natto (fermented soybeans). Within a second or two, you "form an opinion": it's disgusting. Later you also form an opinion that your friend's new partner, who served it, is "a bit untrustworthy."** We will trace this from the 10,000-foot felt opinion down to neurons and back up to the question of whether the opinion is *justified*.

**Layer 0 — The felt surface (10,000 feet).** Phenomenologically, the opinion arrives as a *perception*, not a conclusion. You don't experience "I am now computing an evaluation of natto"; you experience natto *being* gross, the way you experience the bowl *being* on the table. This immediacy is the first clue to the mechanism: opinions feel like perceptions because, mechanistically, they are built by the same machinery.

### NEUROSCIENCE LAYER

**Predictive processing / the prediction machine.** The leading unifying framework in cognitive neuroscience holds that the brain is not a passive receiver of sensory data but an active *prediction machine* (Andy Clark, *Whatever Next?*, *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 2013; *Surfing Uncertainty*; *The Experience Machine*). The brain runs a hierarchical *generative model* that constantly predicts incoming signals; only the mismatch — the *prediction error* — propagates upward to update the model. Anil Seth calls perception a "controlled hallucination": what you experience is the brain's best top-down guess, reined in by sensory evidence. In this view, a belief or opinion is simply a higher, more abstract level of the same predictive hierarchy — a prior expectation about how the world (including social and moral facts) will behave.

*Applied to the example:* Your prior model of "good food" was built from a lifetime of predictions about smell, texture, and taste. Natto's ammonia-like smell and sticky, stringy texture generate a massive prediction error against "edible food," and your brain's fastest resolution is the evaluative output *disgust*.

**The Bayesian brain and the free energy principle.** Karl Friston's free energy principle formalizes this: any self-organizing system that persists must minimize "free energy," a mathematical proxy for *surprise* (prediction error), through approximate Bayesian inference. Friston calls the core imperative "self-evidencing" — the brain acts to gather evidence for its own model of the world. Belief updating is thus literally Bayesian updating: a prior (your existing opinion) is revised by new evidence (the likelihood), weighted by their relative *precision* (confidence). A high-precision (strongly held) prior barely budges; a high-precision surprising signal forces a larger update. This single relation — update ∝ prediction error × precision-weighting — is the mechanistic heart of opinion change.

**Dopamine, prediction error, and reinforcement learning.** The neuromodulator that broadcasts prediction error is dopamine. The classic reward-prediction-error (RPE) hypothesis (Schultz; formalized as temporal-difference learning) holds that midbrain dopamine neurons signal the difference between expected and received reward, updating the value of states and actions via corticostriatal plasticity. Recent work extends this to *belief states*: dopamine reflects updating of probabilistic beliefs under uncertainty (Babayan et al., *Nature Communications*, 2018; Bayesian-inference accounts of nucleus accumbens dopamine). Strikingly, blocking D2/D3 dopamine receptors increases the *volatility* of beliefs when learning whom to trust — direct evidence that dopamine regulates how much new social evidence shifts our opinions of other people.

*Applied to the example:* When you later judge the partner as "untrustworthy," your brain is running social reinforcement learning — every micro-cue (a too-smooth smile, an evasive answer) is a small prediction error about their intentions. Dopaminergic signals weight how much each cue updates your "trust prior." Because your prior was already nudged negative (perhaps by the unpleasant food they chose, a halo/affect spillover), updates are asymmetric.

**Key brain regions (the opinion circuit).** Converging fMRI work implicates:
- **Ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC):** belief acceptance and the integration of emotion/reward into valuation (Harris et al., 2008; Damasio's somatic markers). Harris found belief > disbelief activated vmPFC.
- **Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) and dorsomedial PFC (dmPFC):** conflict detection and *uncertainty* (Harris: uncertainty > belief/disbelief; later work centers an "uncertainty network" on dmPFC, interpreted as "subjective epistemic risk").
- **Insula:** disgust and interoception — central to visceral evaluations (and to the natto reaction specifically).
- **Amygdala:** threat/salience tagging.
- **Striatum/reward circuitry (incl. nucleus accumbens):** value updating and the "reinforcement" of conclusions that feel good.
- **Default mode network:** associated with self-referential, intuitive (System-1-like) thought.

**The neuroscience of motivated reasoning.** Westen, Blagov, Harenski, Kilts & Hamann (2006, *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*, 18(11):1947–1958) scanned 30 committed partisans evaluating threatening information about their preferred 2004 presidential candidate. Motivated reasoning activated the vmPFC, ACC, posterior cingulate, insula, and lateral orbital cortex — and crucially *not* the dorsolateral "cold reasoning" regions. As the Emory release (Jan 31, 2006) put it: "None of the circuits involved in conscious reasoning were particularly engaged... it appears as if partisans twirl the cognitive kaleidoscope until they get the conclusions they want, and then they get massively reinforced for it, with the elimination of negative emotional states and activation of positive ones." This is the neural signature of intuition-first, justification-second.

**Somatic marker hypothesis.** Damasio (*Descartes' Error*, 1994; Bechara & Damasio Iowa Gambling Task) argues that bodily/emotional states ("somatic markers") tag options and candidate beliefs with felt value, rapidly pruning the option space. Without this (as in vmPFC-damaged patients like "EVR"), reasoning becomes endless and decision impossible. The lesson for opinion formation: a felt sense of "this is right/wrong" is not noise added to cognition — it is part of the computation.

### COGNITIVE PSYCHOLOGY LAYER

**Dual-process theory.** Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (building on Stanovich, Evans, Sloman) distinguishes **System 1** (fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional, effortless) from **System 2** (slow, deliberate, analytic, effortful). Most opinions are System-1 outputs; System 2 typically operates in "default-interventionist" mode (Evans & Stanovich) — it can intervene to correct intuitions but usually rationalizes them. (Caveat: leading researchers, including Kahneman, stress these are useful labels, not literal brain modules — Melnikoff & Bargh and others warn against reifying them.)

**Heuristics and biases.** Kahneman & Tversky's program identifies mental shortcuts that generate opinions cheaply: the **availability heuristic** (judging likelihood by ease of recall), **representativeness** (judging by resemblance to a prototype), **anchoring** (over-weighting an initial value), and the **affect heuristic** (letting a global good/bad feeling stand in for analysis). These are System-1 engines of opinion.

**Confirmation bias and myside bias.** Once an opinion exists, people preferentially seek, interpret, and recall confirming evidence. Mercier & Sperber reframe this "myside bias" as the expected output of a reasoning faculty built for *argument*: it is good at finding reasons *for* your side and lazy at evaluating them.

**The argumentative theory of reasoning.** In *The Enigma of Reason* (2017), Mercier and Sperber resolve a "double enigma" (why is reason uniquely human, and why is it so flawed?) by proposing reason evolved for social functions: to *produce* justifications and arguments to persuade others, and to *evaluate* others' arguments. Reason is therefore predictably "biased and lazy" when used alone but can be excellent in well-structured group debate — which is why science (institutionalized adversarial argument) works where lone reasoners (even Linus Pauling on vitamin C) go astray.

**Cognitive dissonance.** Festinger (*A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance*, 1957; *When Prophecy Fails*) showed that holding inconsistent cognitions produces aversive arousal that people reduce by changing an attitude, adding consonant cognitions, or rationalizing. Because behavior is hard to undo, it is usually *beliefs and opinions* that bend to restore consistency — a powerful engine of post-hoc opinion formation.

**Belief perseverance, memory, and the (weak) backfire effect.** Opinions persist even after their evidential basis is discredited (belief perseverance). Memory is *reconstructive*, not a recording — each recall re-renders and can distort, subtly reshaping opinion. An earlier worry was the **backfire effect** (Nyhan & Reifler, 2010: corrections *strengthening* misperceptions). But large replications (Wood & Porter, 2019; subsequent PNAS work) found no corrections capable of triggering backfire across 52 issues: most people update toward facts. The nuance is *attitudinal* vs. *factual* change — people will accept a corrected fact yet keep the broader attitude or policy preference.

*Applied to the example:* Your "natto is disgusting" verdict is an affect-heuristic + representativeness output (it resembles "spoiled food"). If your host says "it's a delicacy, very healthy," dissonance arises; you may resolve it by tasting again (behavior change), by deciding "delicacies aren't for me" (adding a consonant cognition), or by downgrading the host's taste — which feeds the *untrustworthy* judgment. Note belief perseverance: even told natto is safe and nutritious, the visceral opinion lingers.

### SOCIAL PSYCHOLOGY LAYER

**Social identity theory.** Tajfel & Turner (1979) showed via the *minimal group paradigm* that even arbitrary group labels trigger in-group favoritism. People derive self-esteem from group membership, so they adopt and defend the group's opinions as "positive distinctiveness." Opinions thereby become identity markers.

**Conformity and social proof.** Asch's line-judgment experiments (1951–1956) found that, on average, about one-third (~32%) of responses conformed to a unanimous incorrect majority across the 12 critical trials; about 75% of participants conformed at least once while ~25% never conformed, against a control-condition error rate under 1%. Social influence can override the evidence of one's own eyes — a direct demonstration that opinions are partly social products.

**Group polarization and echo chambers.** Discussion among like-minded people pushes the group's average opinion to a more extreme position. Online, selective exposure plus algorithmic filtering ("filter bubbles") and confirmation bias create echo chambers that amplify polarization and a false-consensus sense that one's view is universal.

**Elaboration Likelihood Model.** Petty & Cacioppo (1986) distinguish two persuasion routes: the **central route** (high motivation/ability → deep scrutiny of argument quality → durable, change-resistant attitudes) and the **peripheral route** (low motivation/ability → reliance on cues like source attractiveness, credibility, or sheer number of arguments → fragile attitudes). Which route operates determines how an opinion is formed and how stable it will be.

**Cultural cognition / identity-protective cognition.** Dan Kahan (Cultural Cognition Project, Yale) shows that on culturally contested risks (climate, guns, vaccines, nuclear power), people fit evidence to the position dominant in their group. Disturbingly, *greater* science literacy and numeracy often *increase* polarization (the "motivated numeracy" finding) because cognitively skilled people are better at rationalizing the identity-protective conclusion. Kahan argues this is individually *rational* ("expressive rationality"): for an ordinary citizen, holding the "wrong" view within one's community carries real social costs, while one vote/opinion can't change the policy outcome — so it pays to believe what protects identity.

**Moral foundations and the elephant/rider.** Haidt (*The Righteous Mind*, 2012) argues moral/political opinions are driven by rapid intuitions (the elephant) that reasoning (the rider) then justifies — evidenced by "moral dumbfounding" (people keep a judgment even when they cannot produce any reason for it). His Moral Foundations Theory holds that intuitions are organized around foundations (Care, Fairness/Proportionality, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity, Liberty), likened to moral "taste receptors," with liberals and conservatives weighting them differently. Practical upshot: to change a moral opinion, "talk to the elephant" (intuitions/values), not just the rider (facts).

*Applied to the example:* Suppose the dinner is also a setting where your friend's partner expresses a political view you associate with "the other side." Your distrust now fuses an affective food-disgust spillover, a group-identity signal, and a moral-foundations mismatch. Identity-protective cognition predicts you'll scrutinize their claims harder than an ally's identical claims; ELM predicts that if you're not motivated to engage, you'll judge them on peripheral cues (tone, confidence, likeability) rather than argument quality.

### PHILOSOPHY / EPISTEMOLOGY LAYER

**Opinion vs. belief vs. knowledge.** Classically, *knowledge* is **justified true belief (JTB)**. Gettier (1963, "Is Justified True Belief Knowledge?") showed with counterexamples that one can have a justified true belief that is true only by luck and so isn't knowledge — launching decades of repair attempts (adding a fourth "no false lemmas"/anti-luck condition; reliabilism; etc.). An *opinion* is typically a belief held without claim to certainty or full justification — closer to a judgment or stance than to knowledge.

**Credences and degrees of belief.** Bayesian epistemology models belief not as on/off but as a graded **credence** (subjective probability) obeying the probability axioms, updated by **conditionalization** on new evidence. Foundational work by Frank Ramsey ("Truth and Probability," 1931) and Bruno de Finetti (1937) ties credences to betting behavior and proves (via Dutch Book arguments) that incoherent credences are exploitable. The **Lockean thesis** links the two pictures: "belief" is credence above a threshold. This is the philosophical mirror of the Bayesian brain — the normative theory of how an *ideal* agent should do what the predictive brain does approximately.

**Structure of justification.** *Foundationalism* (beliefs rest on a base of self-justifying foundational beliefs), *coherentism* (beliefs are justified by mutual coherence within a web), and *reliabilism* (a belief is justified if produced by a reliable process, justification need not be accessible to the believer) are the main rival accounts of what makes an opinion *justified*.

**The ethics of belief.** W. K. Clifford ("The Ethics of Belief," 1877) argued "it is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence" (evidentialism). William James ("The Will to Believe," 1896) countered that for "genuine options" that cannot be settled by evidence, our "passional nature" may and must decide. The debate assumes **doxastic voluntarism** — that we can choose beliefs — which Bernard Williams ("Deciding to Believe," 1973) attacked, arguing belief "aims at truth" and so cannot be adopted at will. Most now favor *indirect* voluntarism: we can't believe at will, but we can control what evidence and testimony we expose ourselves to (which loops back to echo chambers and identity-protective cognition).

**Hume, empiricism, and testimony.** Hume's dictum that "reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions" anticipates the elephant/rider and somatic-marker pictures: reason serves motivation. The empiricism/rationalism debate concerns whether ideas originate in experience or innate structure (the modern predictive brain is a hybrid — innate priors shaped by experience). **Social epistemology** stresses that most of what we believe comes from *testimony* (we believe far more than we could ever verify firsthand), and **standpoint epistemology** argues social position shapes epistemic access — both underscoring that opinion formation is irreducibly social.

*Applied to the example:* Is your opinion "natto is bad" *justified*? On reliabilism, your taste system is a fairly reliable detector of *what you will enjoy*, so the opinion "I dislike natto" is justified — but the opinion "natto is bad food" overgeneralizes from a private reaction (it's not knowledge; millions find it delicious). Your "untrustworthy partner" opinion is more epistemically fragile: it rests on thin evidence amplified by affect spillover and possibly identity threat — a Gettier-prone, low-credence judgment that an evidentialist (Clifford) would say you're not entitled to hold firmly.

### DEVELOPMENTAL / EVOLUTIONARY LAYER

**Development.** Opinion-forming capacities scaffold on cognitive milestones: infants show core evaluative reactions (approach/avoid, like/dislike) early; **theory of mind** (understanding that others have beliefs that can differ from one's own and from reality), typically consolidating around ages 3–5 (false-belief tasks), is prerequisite for understanding *opinions as opinions* — i.e., as perspectives that can be right or wrong and can be argued over. Children also acquire opinions heavily via testimony, selectively trusting confident, familiar, and in-group informants.

**Evolutionary origins and why biases can be adaptive.** Error Management Theory (Haselton & Buss, 2000, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 78(1):81–91) holds that when the two possible errors in a judgment have asymmetric costs, natural selection favors a *bias* toward making the cheaper error — even if it means *more* total errors. In their words: "The key point of EMT is that selection will favor biased decision rules that produce more beneficial or less costly outcomes (relative to alternative decision rules), even if those biased rules produce more frequent errors." The canonical analogy is the **smoke-detector principle** (Randolph Nesse, *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 2001; *Evolution and Human Behavior*, 2005): a fire alarm is deliberately biased toward false alarms because a missed fire is catastrophic — "when the cost of expressing an all-or-none defense is low compared to the potential harm it protects against, the optimal system will express many false alarms." Examples include hyperactive agency detection, food-disgust over-avoidance (better to wrongly reject a safe food than eat a poisonous one — directly relevant to the natto reaction), and documented cross-sex misperception biases (men's overperception of women's sexual intent; women's underperception of men's commitment). The upshot: many "irrational" opinion biases are evolved features that minimized fitness costs under uncertainty.

### INTEGRATIVE / GENERAL SCIENCE

**A single spine connects the layers.** The recurring motif — *prior + weighted prediction error → update* — appears as Bayesian inference (philosophy), free-energy minimization and dopaminergic RPE (neuroscience), System-1 intuition corrected (or not) by System 2 (cognitive psychology), and attitude change via central/peripheral routes (social psychology). Opinions are *priors* in this system; they update reluctantly because (a) precise/strong priors mathematically resist revision, (b) updating that threatens the self triggers emotional defense (motivated reasoning), and (c) updating that threatens the group is socially costly (identity-protective cognition). Evolution explains *why* the system is built to be conservatively biased (error management).

**Language and narrative.** Opinions are encoded, transmitted, and rehearsed largely in language and *story*. Narratives package values with facts ("you lead with values to give the facts a fighting chance"), making them potent for the peripheral route and for speaking to "the elephant." Framing effects (Kahneman & Tversky) show that the *wording* of an identical fact changes the opinion it produces.

**Network/contagion models of opinion spread.** Beyond the individual, formal "opinion dynamics" models (sociophysics) treat opinions as states spreading over social networks:
- **DeGroot model** (Morris DeGroot, "Reaching a Consensus," *JASA*, 1974): each agent repeatedly resets its opinion to a fixed weighted average of its neighbors', converging (under connectivity) to a single consensus equal to a centrality-weighted average of initial views.
- **Friedkin–Johnsen model** (1990, *Journal of Mathematical Sociology*, 15:193–206): adds a susceptibility parameter so agents remain partly anchored to their initial "prejudice," producing stable, persistent disagreement (stubborn agents) rather than full consensus.
- **Bounded-confidence models** — **Deffuant–Weisbuch** (Deffuant et al., "Mixing beliefs among interacting agents," *Advances in Complex Systems*, 2000; pairwise/asynchronous) and **Hegselmann–Krause** (*JASSS*, 2002, 5(3); synchronous averaging over all opinions within a bound): agents influence each other *only if their opinions already differ by less than a confidence threshold ε*, producing consensus, polarization, or fragmentation depending on ε.
- **Binary-state physics models** — the **voter model** (Holley & Liggett, *Annals of Probability*, 1975: an agent copies a random neighbor) and the **Sznajd model** (Sznajd-Weron & Sznajd, 2000, "United We Stand, Divided We Fall," where information flows *outward* — an agreeing pair persuades its neighbors).

The standard physics review is Castellano, Fortunato & Loreto, "Statistical physics of social dynamics" (*Reviews of Modern Physics*, 2009, 81:591–646); for bounded confidence specifically, see Lorenz (2007). The bounded-confidence insight is especially illuminating: mathematically, people only update toward those they already find credible — a formal echo of echo chambers and precision-weighting at the population scale.

### Genuine debates and disagreements (no false consensus)
- **Is the free energy principle real science or an unfalsifiable framework?** Critics (including, at times, Anil Seth) note the "prediction machine" view risks "explaining everything and anything" with thin unambiguous empirical support; FEP is mathematically elegant but its empirical content is contested.
- **Are System 1 and System 2 real?** Many researchers treat them as a useful metaphor, not distinct neural systems (Melnikoff & Bargh); some evolutionary psychologists doubt a domain-general System 2 exists.
- **Does dopamine encode pure prediction error, or precision/uncertainty too?** Active debate (RPE vs. Bayesian/precision-weighting accounts; recent data showing dopamine RPEs independent of learning rate).
- **How general is identity-protective cognition / motivated numeracy?** Kahan's strong claims are debated; the backfire effect's collapse suggests people are more responsive to facts than the gloomiest accounts imply.
- **Doxastic voluntarism:** unresolved whether we have any direct control over belief.
- **Is reasoning's function really argumentative?** Mercier & Sperber's thesis is influential but contested (e.g., on the status of solitary deductive reasoning).

## Recommendations

**If your goal is to build the layered mental model (the user's stated aim):**
1. **Anchor the whole model on one sentence:** *"The brain guesses first and justifies second; an opinion is a prior that updates only in proportion to surprise × confidence, discounted by how much the update threatens the self and the group."* Use it as the through-line at every zoom level.
2. **Structure the explainer as five nested "zoom" levels** using the natto/trust example: (0) felt opinion → (1) intuition-first dual process → (2) prediction error + dopamine + vmPFC/insula → (3) motivated reasoning + dissonance → (4) social identity/cultural cognition → (5) epistemic justification (is it knowledge? what's your credence?). End each level by re-asking "...but *why*?" to motivate the next.
3. **Build the glossary** from the precise terms flagged here: prediction error, precision-weighting, generative model, free energy/self-evidencing, reward prediction error, somatic marker, System 1/2, availability/representativeness/anchoring/affect heuristics, myside/confirmation bias, cognitive dissonance, belief perseverance, social identity, identity-protective cognition, expressive rationality, elaboration likelihood (central/peripheral), moral foundations, moral dumbfounding, JTB, Gettier case, credence, conditionalization, Lockean thesis, foundationalism/coherentism/reliabilism, doxastic voluntarism, evidentialism, error management theory, smoke-detector principle, bounded confidence.

**If your goal is to change opinions (yours or others'):**
4. **Lower the identity stakes first.** Because the binding constraint is usually threat, not information, affirm the person's identity/values *before* presenting disconfirming evidence; self-affirmation measurably reduces defensive processing. Benchmark: if the topic is identity-fused (climate, politics, group loyalties), assume facts alone will fail and lead with shared values ("talk to the elephant").
5. **Engineer good argumentative conditions.** Since reasoning excels in adversarial-but-cooperative groups, seek out the *strongest* opposing view and structure genuine back-and-forth rather than solo deliberation.
6. **Use the central route for durability.** If you want an opinion to *stick* and resist counter-persuasion, build it via deep engagement with argument quality, not peripheral cues.
7. **For your own beliefs, exploit indirect doxastic control.** You can't will a belief, but you *can* choose your information diet — diversify sources to defeat bounded-confidence/echo-chamber dynamics, and pre-commit to updating on specified evidence.

**Thresholds that should change the above:** If new, well-powered studies show a real backfire effect for a given population/topic, revert to extreme caution about repeating misinformation. If a person's opinion is *not* identity-fused (e.g., a factual or consumer judgment), skip the values-first approach and lead directly with strong evidence — central-route persuasion works fine when stakes to identity are low.

## Caveats
- **Levels of description, not competing truths.** Neuroscience, psychology, and philosophy describe the same process at different grains; "the vmPFC does it," "the person is defending their identity," and "the credence is unjustified" are complementary, not rival, claims. Avoid greedy reductionism (neurons "explaining away" justification) and its opposite.
- **fMRI caution.** Reverse inference ("region X lit up, therefore process Y") is a known pitfall; the Westen (n=30) and Harris (n=14) studies are influential but use small samples and early-2000s methods. Treat region-to-function mappings as suggestive, not definitive.
- **Replication and era.** Several pillars predate the replication crisis. Dual-process theory, some priming results, and related ideas have been challenged; the backfire effect itself shows how a "known" finding can dissolve. The intuition-first core, however, is robust across paradigms.
- **The free energy principle is contested** as to whether it is an empirical theory or a mathematical framework; cite it as a unifying *lens*, not settled fact.
- **WEIRD samples.** Much of this literature rests on Western, educated participants; moral-foundations weightings and conformity rates vary cross-culturally.
- **Normative vs. descriptive.** Bayesian epistemology says how an ideal agent *should* update; the brain only approximates this and is systematically, adaptively biased. Don't conflate the two.
