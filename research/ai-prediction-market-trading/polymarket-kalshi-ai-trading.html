The whole game in one line: a YES share pays $1 if the event happens and $0 if not, so its price p is the crowd's probability. You only profit when your own estimate q beats it. Everything else — Kelly, arbitrage, agents — is machinery around q − p.

A From ELI5 → Expert in 5 steps

🧒 ELI5 🧑‍🎓 Beginner 💼 Intermediate 🧪 Advanced 🔬 Expert
Level 1 — ELI5
It's a betting slip worth $1 if you're right, $0 if you're wrong. If lots of people think something will happen, the slip costs more — like 80¢. You only win money when you think the real chance is higher than what the slip costs. That's the whole game. Everything else is just machinery around that one idea.
Level 2 — Beginner
The price of a YES share (say 0.65) is the market saying “65% chance.” Buy it only if you think the true chance q is above 0.65. Your average profit per share is q − p. Do that many times and the math works in your favor — if your guesses are actually good.
Level 3 — Intermediate
You size bets with Kelly: f* = (q−p)/(1−p) of your bankroll, but use only ¼–½ of it because your q has estimation error. You diversify across uncorrelated markets so variance falls (Grinold's Law: skill × √breadth = your Sharpe ratio). And you grab arbitrage when YES + NO < $1 — that's as close to free money as it gets.
Level 4 — Advanced
Forecasting (make q > p) and execution (capture it without ruin) are separable skills. Score q with Brier / log-loss; update it with Bayes; respect microstructure (Kyle λ, adverse selection) and resolution risk (UMA oracle disputes, capital lock-up). Correlation — not individual edge — is what actually blows accounts up. Twenty “different” election markets are one bet wearing twenty hats.
Level 5 — Expert
Edge is a systems problem: reinforcing efficiency loops kill alpha in deep liquid markets, so it concentrates in niche / newly-listed / ambiguous / illiquid markets and in the seconds after news before the book reprices. The legitimate “auto-research” loop is a calibration-feedback loop that optimises measured Brier — not research volume, which Prediction Arena showed is uncorrelated with returns. Engineer a portfolio: arb floor + √N breadth + fractional-Kelly + correlation caps + Brier-graded self-improvement.

C The finding the hype skips

The only real-capital benchmark of autonomous AI traders lost money. In Prediction Arena, six frontier models traded real $10k accounts on Kalshi & Polymarket for 57 days. Every model finished −16.0% to −30.8% on Kalshi — and the paper found research volume had little correlation with returns. The bottleneck isn't generating ideas; it's calibration and monetizing a correct view.
Edge is brutally concentrated. ~67% of Polymarket profit goes to the top 0.1% of accounts (WSJ). The top-10 wallets hold ~21% of all profit and are ~0.07% of accounts (0xinsider).
The biggest winner was human, not a bot. France's "Théo" made ~$48–85M on Trump 2024 from a contrarian thesis that polls undercounted Trump — not an LLM loop (PolyTrack).
Shared "profitable bots" usually don't replicate. A viral "+39% Kalshi World Cup bot (with code)" was rebuilt by a commenter who reported "IT DOESN'T WORK!!"; others called the repo a coin-stealer. Community law: "if you had a money-printer, why post it on Reddit?"

I Risks & open questions (the via-negativa list)

  • Efficiency decay — deep liquid markets are arbed; edge lives in niche/new/ambiguous/illiquid markets and the seconds after news.
  • Replication problem — shared strategies rarely replicate; many repos are scams.
  • Oracle/resolution risk — UMA disputes, ambiguous wording, weeks-long capital lock-up.
  • Regulation & insider info — some "edge" is illegal private info; CFTC now hunts it with ML; US-person Polymarket access is restricted.
  • Capacity — arbitrage & microstructure edges are small, competed away at scale.
  • The core caution — autonomous frontier models lost in the only real-capital test, and "more research" didn't help.
